Problem Definition

The Core Challenge

  • The Issue: While top-line gross transaction value is growing across our global distribution network, net profit margins are being aggressively eroded.
  • The Friction: High customer acquisition costs from channels like Paid Ads and Social Media are compounded by excessive promotional discounting and high product return rates.
  • The Gap: The business lacks an automated, predictive system to identify which combinations of product categories, traffic sources, and pricing discount structures trigger unprofitable transactions.

Business & Financial Impact

  • Margin Compression: Heavy discounts (discount_percent) drive high volume (quantity) but cannibalize the net profit per order.
  • Logistical Drain: Processing returns (is_returned) creates major operational bottlenecks and sunk costs via absorbed shipping_cost overhead.
  • The Goal: Develop an exploratory and machine learning framework to optimize marketing spend allocation, predict order return probabilities, and maximize net profit margins.

Importance of the Topic

Why This Research Matters

  • Online retailers invest heavily in customer acquisition, but not every marketing channel generates equally valuable customers.
  • Discounts can increase sales, but excessive discounting may reduce profitability.
  • Understanding how acquisition channels and discount levels influence customer value helps businesses make more effective marketing decisions.
  • Predictive models allow retailers to identify future high-value customers and improve marketing performance.

Practical Applications

  • Prioritize higher-performing acquisition channels.
  • Optimize discount strategies to improve profitability.
  • Reduce unnecessary marketing expenses.
  • Improve customer targeting and retention.
  • Support data-driven business decision making.

RO2: Customer Satisfaction Analysis (Alexa)

RO2: Research Objective

Objective: Identify which customer and transaction characteristics contribute to higher customer satisfaction by examining how product categories, traffic sources, discount levels, and purchasing behavior influence customer rating.

  • The Hypothesis: Customers who purchase through Organic or Direct traffic sources, receive moderate discounts, and purchase from higher-rated product categories will report higher customer satisfaction scores (rating) than customers acquired through paid channels or heavily discounted purchases.

  • Key Customer Satisfaction Indicators:

    • Overall customer rating following a purchase.
    • Product categories associated with higher customer satisfaction.
    • Traffic sources that generate more satisfied customers.
    • The relationship between promotional discounts and customer satisfaction.

RO2: Methodology & Statistical Modeling

  • Analytical Approach in R:
    • Perform Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) using the tidyverse package to summarize customer satisfaction across products, marketing channels, and purchasing behaviors.
    • Fit a Multiple Linear Regression Model using the baseline lm() function to determine which customer and transaction characteristics significantly influence customer rating.
    • Use the broom package to organize regression coefficients, confidence intervals, and statistical significance for interpretation.
  • Model Validation:
    • Examine residual plots to verify regression assumptions.
    • Evaluate overall model performance using R² and Adjusted R².
    • Identify statistically significant predictors through p-values and confidence intervals.

RO3: Returns & Profitability Analysis

RO3: Research Objective

Objective: Evaluate how product categories, shipping costs, customer ratings, and discount structures impact the probability of an item being returned (is_returned), and quantify the resulting drain on net profit.

  • The Hypothesis: High discount rates (discount_percent > 30%) combined with low customer satisfaction scores (rating < 3.0) in specific categories like Fashion and Footwear are the primary drivers of item returns.
  • Key Risk Indicators:
    • Post-purchase return behavior patterns.
    • Sunk shipping costs absorbed during returned transactions.
    • Sentiment/Rating thresholds that trigger a return.

RO3: Methodology & Statistical Modeling

  • Analytical Approach in R:
    • Fit a Logistic Regression Model using the baseline glm(..., family = binomial) function to isolate probabilities.
    • Use the broom package to clean up statistical outputs and extract odds ratios for our predictors.
  • Model Validation:
    • Split data into training/testing sets using caret.
    • Evaluate performance using Confusion Matrices, ROC curves (pROC package), and Area Under the Curve (AUC) metrics.

RO4: Profit Impact of Customer Retention

RO4: Research Objective

Objective: Determine whether repeat customers are a more margin-efficient growth channel than new customer acquisition

  • The Hypothesis: Repeat customers are a more margin-efficient growth channel than new customer acquisition, generating higher cumulative profit and lower return rates than one-time customers, and are disproportionately acquired through lower-cost channels (Organic, Direct, Email) rather than paid channels (Paid Ads, Social Media).

    • % of a customer’s orders that were returned

Conclusion

Bringing It Together

  • We set out to explain why gross transaction value is growing while net profit margins erode — and each research objective isolates a different piece of that puzzle
  • Acquisition (RO1): No single traffic source drives the highest AOV overall, but the best-performing channel shifts by product category — pointing to a category-specific acquisition strategy
  • Satisfaction (RO2): Customer rating isn’t just a feedback metric — if channel, discount level, and category predict satisfaction, it becomes an early-warning signal for the return risk RO3 measures downstream
  • Risk & operations (RO3): Return risk appears concentrated where high discounting and low ratings intersect, directly linking promotional strategy to the “logistical drain” named in our problem statement
  • Retention (RO4): If repeat customers prove more margin-efficient and disproportionately organic/low-cost-acquired, that reframes retention as a profitability lever, not just a loyalty metric
  • Timing (RO5): Seasonal patterns by category inform when to spend, not just where — completing the picture alongside RO1’s channel findings

What This Means for the Business

  • Marketing spend, discount policy, satisfaction, and retention are not separate problems — they interact, and treating them separately is likely part of why margins keep compressing despite top-line growth
  • A predictive framework combining these findings could flag which channel–category–discount combinations are heading toward an unprofitable, low-satisfaction, or high-return transaction before it happens
  • This directly answers the gap named in our problem definition: the business currently lacks that kind of automated, predictive view

Next Steps

  • Next step: move from exploratory/descriptive analysis (this proposal) into the predictive modeling stage — logistic regression for return risk (RO3), multiple regression for satisfaction drivers (RO2), and potentially extending both frameworks to predict order-level profitability directly
